Introduction

The RMS Titanic, a marvel of engineering and luxury, set sail on its maiden voyage from Southampton, England to New York City on April 10, 1912. Carrying over 2,200 passengers and crew of diverse social and economic backgrounds, the Titanic was the largest ship of its time and was considered unsinkable. From the opulent First-Class cabins to the modest Third-Class acommodations, the Titanic was a microcosm of early 20th-century society, showcasing stark socio-economic divides.

But when disaster struck, these divides were more than just symbolic--they directly influenced survival rates. The question of who survived and who perished was not just a matter of chance; it was shaped by class, wealth, age, gender, and other demographics.

Understanding Socio-Economic Disparity

The Titanic was a reflection of Edwardian society, where class and privilege dictated outcomes in life and death. The wealthiest first-class passengers enjoyed luxurious accommodations and priority access to lifeboats, resulting in a survival rate of 64%, compared to just 24% of third-class passengers, who were largely confined to the lower decks. This stark divide mirrored the rigid social hierarchy of the time, where economic status determined one's opportunities and chances of survival. The Titanic's tragic fate exposed the harsh inequalities of an era that valued wealth and social standing above all else.

Insights from the Network Graph: Class, Gender, and Survival Outcomes

Network analysis shows that gender played a critical role in survival. Among all female passengers, 74% survived, while only 19% of male passengers survived. This disparity reflects evacuation policies that prioritized women over men, especially in first and second classes. The network graph highlights these relationships, emphasizing that wealth and gender influenced survival chances.

Insights from the Line Graph: Survival Rates by Age Range

The line graph displaying survival rates by age range highlights significant trends. Children aged 0-10 had the highest survival rate of 60%, reflecting the "women and children first" policy. In contrast, passengers aged 61-70 had the lowest survival rate at just 20%, indicating that older passengers faced significant disadvantages during the evacuation and rescue efforts.

Insights from the Radar Chart: Comparison of Survival by Class

The radar chart compares survival rates, average age, and average fare of passengers across all classes. First-class passengers paid the highest fares and were generally older, with an average age of approximately 39 years, compared to second-class passengers averaging 29 years and third-class passengers averaging 24 years. Survival rates mirror socio-economic divides, with first-class passengers surviving at more than twice the rate of third-class passengers.

Society and Survival: Lessons from the Titanic

When the Titanic sank on April 15, 1912, only 700 our of more than 2,200 passengers survived. Despite third-class passengers making up 55% of the total passenger population, only 24% of the survivors cames from third-class while 63% of first-class passengers survived. Women and children, especially in upper classes, had significantly higher chances of survival, reflecting the "women and children first" policy. Meanwhile, third-class men and older passengers faced overwhelming disadvantages, with survival rates plummeting to as low as 20% for some age groups.

The embarkation points reveal the diverse backgrounds of those abroad, from wealthy elites departing Cherbourg to hopeful immigrants embarking Queenstown. The Titanic, as a reflection of its time, laid bare the harsh realities of wealth inequality and social hierarchy, where one's fate was determined by their socio-economic status. Through these data visualizations, we gain not only a clearer understanding of the tragic loss of life but also a deeper appreciation for the human stories behind the statistics.
