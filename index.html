Introduction

The RMS Titanic, a marvel of engineering and luxury, set sail on its maiden voyage from Southampton, England to New York City on April 10, 1912. Carrying over 2,200 passengers and crew of diverse social and economic backgrounds, the Titanic was the largest ship of its time and was considered unsinkable. From the opulent First-Class cabins to the modest Third-Class acommodations, the Titanic was a microcosm of early 20th-century society, showcasing stark socio-economic divides.

But when disaster struck, these divides were more than just symbolic--they directly influenced survival rates. The question of who survived and who perished was not just a matter of chance; it was shaped by class, wealth, age, gender, and other demographics.
